The Journey of David Coolidge

Punk Rock, Islam, and Self-Discovery: David Coolidge's Unique Journey

When punk rock music intertwines with Islam, it leads individuals like David Coolidge on a path of self-discovery, faith, and scholarship. David's background in Chicago and his pursuit of a PhD in Islamic studies exemplify the diverse experiences of Muslims in America, challenging common assumptions. David Coolidge, born in Chicago, Illinois, and now residing in Oakland, California, embarked on a journey of self-discovery and faith. In 1998, while attending Brown University in Rhode Island, David converted to Islam after being introduced to the religion by two members of the Chicago punk rock music community who had also converted. Intrigued by religion in general, David's friends took him to an Islamic bookstore on Devon Street, Chicago's Indian-Pakistani area, where he acquired a Quran and other books on Islam. This experience eventually led him to pursue a PhD in Islamic studies, highlighting the diverse backgrounds and experiences of Muslims in America.

David Coolidge recently completed his PhD in Islamic studies, focusing on how Muslims perceive the Hindu tradition both historically and in the present. His dissertation explored a specific branch of the Hindu tradition that emerged 500 years ago in the Bengal Sultanate under Muslim rule, which has now evolved into a global tradition with converts worldwide. This research demonstrates how the Hindu tradition continues to grow, change, and adapt over time. Although historical Muslim perspectives on the Hindu tradition are fascinating, they may not always be relevant to the modern context, as Hinduism has become a global tradition with temples in cities like San Francisco and converts sharing their stories.

Growing up in a liberal Protestant church, David Coolidge experienced various religious traditions. His church, located near his parents' house, held Sunday school and youth group activities. One significant tradition in David's church was confirmation, where individuals confirmed the grace of their baptism as adults, affirming their beliefs. Participants were required to recite the Apostles' Creed, an ancient, widespread Christian statement of faith, in front of the entire congregation. This process involved either memorizing the creed or reading it from a piece of paper.

From Curiosity to Conversion and Exploration of Islamic Beliefs

David Coolidge's interest in religion led him to talk to his acquaintances who had converted to Islam, as they were his only connection to Muslims at the time. Their first meeting took place at a Middle Eastern restaurant in Chicago. However, David struggled to understand one of the guys, as he was using many Arabic phrases. It was only later that he realized that these phrases were commonly used in Arabic. Despite the initial language barrier, this meeting marked the beginning of David's exploration of Islam and his eventual journey towards converting to the faith.

David Coolidge's curiosity about Islam led him to ask his acquaintances for books on the subject. They took him to a bookstore where he purchased a Quran and a few other books. After reading them all within a week, he found that, unlike other religious traditions he had encountered, Islam resonated with him as potentially true. While other traditions had interesting and beautiful aspects, they didn't seem as plausible to him as Islam did. This marked a significant turning point in David's exploration of the faith.

David Coolidge initially saw his conversion to Islam as simply entering the faith without affiliating with a specific branch. It wasn't until he furthered his studies over the years that he gained an understanding of the historical development and the different branches within Islam, such as Sunni and Shia. This allowed him to gain deeper insight into the various perspectives and practices present in the Islamic tradition.

Upon converting to Islam, David Coolidge found himself adopting a conservative, Salafi, Islamist perspective that was influenced by various Islamic movements and organizations. His beliefs were a blend of moderate Salafism, the Muslim Brotherhood in Egypt, Jama'ati Islami in Pakistan led by Maulana Maududi, and the teachings of popular scholars from the Islamic Society of North America (ISNA) at the time. His understanding of the faith had a strong political edge, with discussions focused on conflicts in Chechnya, Kashmir, the southern Philippines, and Palestine. David saw the Muslim community as a single Ummah with borderlands where jihad was taking place, and he identified with the Muslims fighting against the disbelievers. This perspective also led him to advocate for the establishment of an Islamic state governed by Sharia law.

David Coolidge's beliefs also included support for Hizb ut-Tahrir, a group aiming to reestablish the Sunni caliphate. He distinctly remembered his first visit to the Ummah room at DePaul University, where he saw a poster quoting a hadith from Musnad Ahmed ibn Hanbal. The hadith described a righteous caliphate eventually giving way to kingdoms and disarray, but ultimately reemerging as a righteous caliphate in the end. This reinforced David's belief in the importance of working towards an Islamic state governed by Sharia law.
